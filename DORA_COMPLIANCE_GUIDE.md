# DORA Compliance Guide for ICT Third-Party Service Providers

## Executive Summary

The **Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA)** (Regulation EU 2022/2554) is a comprehensive EU regulation establishing digital operational resilience requirements for the financial sector. DORA becomes applicable on **January 17, 2025**.

This guide focuses on Articles 28-30, which govern ICT third-party risk management—the most critical requirements for ICT service providers serving financial entities.

## Overview

### What is DORA?

DORA creates a unified regulatory framework across all EU member states for:
- **ICT risk management** (Articles 5-16)
- **ICT-related incident management and reporting** (Articles 17-23)
- **Digital operational resilience testing** (Articles 24-27)
- **ICT third-party risk management** (Articles 28-30)
- **Oversight framework for critical ICT third-party providers** (Articles 31-44)

### Who Does DORA Apply To?

**Financial Entities:**
- Credit institutions (banks)
- Investment firms
- Payment institutions
- Electronic money institutions
- Insurance and reinsurance undertakings
- Pension funds
- Investment funds and managers
- Crypto-asset service providers

**ICT Third-Party Service Providers:**
- Cloud computing providers
- Software providers
- Data analytics providers
- Cybersecurity service providers
- Any provider supporting critical ICT functions for financial entities

## Timeline

- **Publication Date:** December 27, 2022
- **Application Date:** January 17, 2025
- **Regulatory Technical Standards (RTS):** Being finalized by EBA, ESMA, EIOPA throughout 2024

## Articles 28-30: ICT Third-Party Risk Management

### Article 28: General Requirements

#### Article 28(1): Risk Management Framework
Financial entities must manage ICT third-party risk as part of their ICT risk management framework.

**Provider Implications:**
- Demonstrate documented ICT risk management processes
- Align with financial entity risk management requirements
- Provide evidence of risk controls and mitigation measures

**Evidence Required:**
- ICT third-party risk management policy
- Due diligence procedures
- Risk assessment methodologies
- Board/management oversight documentation

#### Article 28(2): Risk Assessment Requirements

**Pre-Contractual Assessment:**
- Comprehensive due diligence before engagement
- Assessment of provider's operational resilience
- Evaluation of concentration risk
- Analysis of subcontracting arrangements

**Ongoing Monitoring:**
- Periodic risk reassessment
- Continuous monitoring of provider performance
- Tracking changes in risk profile
- Concentration risk analysis

**Provider Actions:**
- Support client due diligence processes
- Provide risk assessment information
- Disclose concentration risk factors
- Report material changes promptly

#### Article 28(3): Contractual Arrangements

All ICT third-party relationships must be governed by written contracts with specific mandatory provisions (detailed in Article 29).

**Key Requirements:**
- Written contractual agreements (no verbal arrangements)
- Clear definition of rights and obligations
- Compliance with DORA Article 29 mandatory clauses
- Regular contract review and updates

#### Article 28(4): Exit Strategies

Financial entities must establish exit strategies for all ICT third-party arrangements.

**Provider Obligations:**
- Develop comprehensive exit/transition plans
- Ensure data portability in commonly used formats
- Support business continuity during transitions
- Provide transition assistance post-termination
- Enable complete and certified data deletion

**Exit Plan Components:**
- Transition timeline and milestones
- Data extraction procedures
- Knowledge transfer processes
- Service continuity arrangements
- Parallel operation support

### Article 29: Key Contractual Provisions

Article 29(1) specifies **mandatory contractual clauses** for ICT service agreements:

#### Article 29(1)(a): Service Description
- Clear and complete description of ICT services
- Service levels and performance metrics
- Geographic locations of service delivery
- Specific functions and capabilities provided

#### Article 29(1)(b): Data Locations
- All locations where data is processed
- All locations where data is stored
- Include subcontractor and cloud provider locations
- Notification requirements for location changes

#### Article 29(1)(c): Service Level Agreements
- Quantitative and qualitative service level targets
- Availability requirements (uptime commitments)
- Recovery Time Objectives (RTO)
- Recovery Point Objectives (RPO)
- Performance monitoring and measurement mechanisms
- Regular reporting on SLA compliance

#### Article 29(1)(d): Audit Rights
- Full access and audit rights for financial entities
- Access rights for competent authorities
- Inspection rights for supervisors
- Right to obtain information on ICT risk management
- Third-party certification and audit reports

**Authority Access:**
Financial entities' supervisory authorities have the right to directly access and audit ICT third-party providers (Article 29(5)).

#### Article 29(1)(e): Subcontracting
- Requirement for prior written approval for subcontracting
- Notification of all subcontractors
- Flow-down of contractual obligations to subcontractors
- Equivalent security and resilience standards for subcontractors
- Financial entity right to object to subcontractors

#### Article 29(1)(f): Incident Notification
- Immediate notification of ICT-related incidents
- Classification criteria for incident severity
- Notification timelines aligned with DORA Article 19
- Details on impact, root cause, and remediation
- Regular incident reporting

#### Article 29(1)(g): Exit Clauses
- Termination rights for financial entities
- Termination for cause (including regulatory non-compliance)
- Notice periods and termination procedures
- Transition assistance obligations
- Post-termination support commitments

#### Article 29(1)(h): Data Security Requirements
- Comprehensive data security and protection measures
- Encryption requirements (in transit and at rest)
- Access control mechanisms
- Data segregation (logical and/or physical)
- Protection against unauthorized access
- Data lifecycle security

#### Article 29(2): Performance Monitoring
- Regular performance reporting to financial entities
- SLA compliance reporting
- Service metrics and KPIs
- Trend analysis

#### Article 29(3): Designated Contacts
- Qualified personnel as relationship managers
- Points of contact for escalations
- Dedicated support resources

#### Article 29(4): Regulatory Adaptability
- Provisions to adapt to regulatory changes
- Flexibility for evolving supervisory expectations
- Contract amendment procedures for compliance updates

#### Article 29(5): Supervisory Authority Access
- Explicit contractual grant of access rights to competent authorities
- No restrictions on authority inspection rights
- Cooperation with supervisory oversight

### Article 30: Register of Information

Financial entities must maintain a **register** documenting all ICT third-party arrangements.

#### Article 30(1): Register Maintenance
Financial entities maintain the register, but providers must **support** accurate registration.

**Provider Support:**
- Provide accurate information for registration
- Update information when changes occur
- Maintain own records for verification

#### Article 30(2): Information Requirements

The register must include:

**(a) Third-Party Provider Information:**
- Legal name and entity details
- Corporate structure
- Geographic locations
- Contact information

**(b) Contractual Relationship:**
- Contract start and end dates
- Notice periods
- Renewal terms
- Termination clauses

**(c) Services Provided:**
- Description of ICT services
- Criticality classification (critical vs. non-critical)
- Functions supported
- Dependencies

**(d) Data Processing:**
- Types of data processed
- Data locations (all geographies)
- Processing activities
- Data protection measures

**(e) Subcontractors:**
- Identification of all subcontractors
- Subcontractor locations
- Subcontracted functions
- Fourth-party dependencies

**(f) Countries:**
- All countries where data is stored or processed
- Cross-border data flow documentation

**(g) Business Criticality:**
- Assessment of service criticality
- Impact analysis if service unavailable
- Alternative arrangements

**(h) Substitutability:**
- Availability of alternative providers
- Ease of substitution
- Lock-in factors

#### Article 30(3): Register Updates
- Register must be updated at least annually
- Event-driven updates for material changes
- Prompt notification of changes to financial entities

**Material Changes Requiring Notification:**
- Subcontractor additions or changes
- Data location changes
- Service modifications
- Corporate structure changes
- Security incident impacts

## Assessment Workflow

### For Financial Entities Using This Questionnaire:

1. **Pre-Engagement Phase:**
   - Send DORA questionnaire to prospective providers
   - Review responses and evidence
   - Conduct risk assessment based on responses
   - Make engagement decision

2. **Due Diligence Phase:**
   - Validate questionnaire responses
   - Review evidence documents
   - Conduct on-site assessments (if applicable)
   - Assess criticality of proposed services

3. **Contracting Phase:**
   - Ensure contract includes Article 29 mandatory clauses
   - Negotiate SLAs aligned with organizational requirements
   - Establish audit and access rights
   - Define exit strategy

4. **Ongoing Monitoring Phase:**
   - Periodic reassessment (at least annually)
   - Performance monitoring against SLAs
   - Incident tracking and reporting
   - Register maintenance

### For ICT Service Providers Completing This Questionnaire:

1. **Preparation:**
   - Conduct internal DORA readiness assessment
   - Gap analysis against questionnaire requirements
   - Gather evidence documents
   - Involve legal, compliance, IT, and security teams

2. **Completion:**
   - Answer all required questions honestly
   - Provide detailed, specific responses
   - Attach supporting evidence
   - Highlight areas of DORA alignment

3. **Remediation:**
   - Address identified gaps
   - Implement missing controls
   - Update policies and procedures
   - Obtain necessary certifications

4. **Submission:**
   - Quality review before submission
   - Include all required evidence
   - Be prepared for follow-up questions
   - Support validation activities

## Integration with NIS2 Directive

DORA and the **NIS2 Directive** (Directive EU 2022/2555) have overlapping requirements, particularly for:

### Overlapping Areas:
- **ICT Risk Management:** Both require comprehensive risk management frameworks
- **Incident Reporting:** Similar notification timelines and requirements
- **Supply Chain Security:** Both address third-party and supplier risks
- **Governance:** Management responsibility for cybersecurity and resilience

### Key Differences:
- **Scope:** DORA is sector-specific (financial); NIS2 is cross-sectoral
- **Applicability:** DORA applies directly; NIS2 requires national transposition
- **Supervision:** DORA has EU-level oversight; NIS2 has national competent authorities
- **Penalties:** Different penalty structures

### Recommended Approach:
- Develop integrated compliance program covering both DORA and NIS2
- Map requirements to identify overlaps and gaps
- Implement harmonized controls satisfying both regulations
- Coordinate incident reporting to meet both timelines
- Align governance and risk management frameworks

## Critical vs. Non-Critical ICT Service Providers

### Critical ICT Third-Party Service Providers

Providers designated as **critical** (under DORA Articles 31-40) face additional requirements:

**Designation Criteria:**
- Single point of failure for multiple financial entities
- Difficult or impossible to substitute
- Significant systemic risk if service disrupted
- Designation by Lead Oversight Authority (LOA)

**Additional Obligations:**
- Direct supervision by European Supervisory Authorities (ESAs)
- Payment of supervisory fees
- Submission to on-site inspections
- Enhanced reporting requirements
- Formal oversight framework compliance

**If Designated Critical:**
- Receive formal notification from ESA
- Develop oversight compliance program
- Establish ESA liaison procedures
- Budget for supervisory fees
- Prepare for direct supervision

### Non-Critical Providers

Most ICT providers will not be designated as critical but must still:
- Comply with Article 28 requirements
- Include Article 29 mandatory clauses in contracts
- Support financial entity register maintenance (Article 30)
- Provide audit access to financial entities and authorities

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### For Providers:

1. **Inadequate Documentation:** Generic or boilerplate responses without specific evidence
2. **Unrealistic SLAs:** Commitments that cannot be met operationally
3. **Subcontractor Opacity:** Failing to disclose full subcontracting chain
4. **Data Location Ambiguity:** Vague statements about data residency
5. **Weak Exit Planning:** No practical exit strategy or data portability
6. **Limited Audit Rights:** Restricting financial entity or authority access
7. **Delayed Incident Notification:** Not meeting DORA timelines
8. **Regulatory Inflexibility:** Contracts that cannot adapt to regulatory changes
9. **Insufficient Evidence:** Claims without supporting documentation
10. **Ignoring Concentration Risk:** Not assessing or reporting dependencies

### For Financial Entities:

1. **Incomplete Assessments:** Not validating provider responses
2. **Weak Contracts:** Missing Article 29 mandatory clauses
3. **Poor Register Maintenance:** Outdated or incomplete third-party register
4. **Concentration Risk Blindness:** Not identifying cumulative dependencies
5. **Exit Strategy Neglect:** No tested exit plans for critical providers
6. **Monitoring Gaps:** Inadequate ongoing oversight of providers
7. **Authority Access Issues:** Not ensuring contractual rights for supervisors

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Q1: Does DORA apply to non-EU ICT service providers?
**A:** Yes, if you provide ICT services to EU financial entities, DORA applies regardless of your location.

### Q2: What happens if I don't comply with DORA by January 17, 2025?
**A:** Financial entities may be prohibited from engaging non-compliant providers. Critical providers face ESA sanctions.

### Q3: Do I need separate contracts for each financial entity client?
**A:** Yes, each contractual relationship must include Article 29 mandatory clauses, though you can use standardized templates.

### Q4: Can I refuse to grant audit rights to competent authorities?
**A:** No, Article 29(5) requires explicit contractual grant of access rights to supervisory authorities without restriction.

### Q5: How often must I reassess my DORA compliance?
**A:** At minimum annually, plus event-driven reassessment for material changes.

### Q6: What are the penalties for DORA non-compliance?
**A:** Financial entities face penalties for engaging non-compliant providers. Critical providers may face ESA sanctions including fines.

### Q7: Does DORA replace GDPR for data protection?
**A:** No, DORA and GDPR are complementary. Both apply, and you must comply with both.

### Q8: Can I use subcontractors without notifying financial entity clients?
**A:** No, Article 29(1)(e) requires prior written approval for subcontracting critical functions.

### Q9: What is the difference between DORA and ISO 27001?
**A:** ISO 27001 is a voluntary standard; DORA is mandatory regulation. However, ISO 27001 certification demonstrates many DORA control requirements.

### Q10: How do I know if I'll be designated as a critical provider?
**A:** The Lead Oversight Authority will formally notify you. Designation considers substitutability, systemic importance, and impact.

## Conclusion

DORA represents a significant shift in how ICT third-party risk is managed in the EU financial sector. By understanding and implementing the requirements of Articles 28-30, ICT service providers can:

- **Build trust** with financial entity clients
- **Demonstrate resilience** and operational excellence
- **Differentiate** in a competitive market
- **Prepare** for potential critical provider designation
- **Reduce regulatory risk** for clients and themselves

Start your DORA compliance journey early, engage with your financial entity clients proactively, and view DORA as an opportunity to strengthen your operational resilience and market position.
